Research

Behavioral Science of Data Visualization

My research examines how data visualizations shape the way people interpret evidence, evaluate information, update beliefs, form opinions, and act online. Across three connected works in progress, I study visual design as a behavioral input: not only as a way to communicate data, but as a set of cues that can make evidence feel clearer, more demanding, more credible, or more persuasive.

Visual Design Features as Persuasive Cues. This stream studies how features of real interactive news visualizations shape perceptions, attitudes, and online behavior. Methodologically, it combines feature coding of 130 COVID-19 visualizations, dimensionality reduction through Factor Analysis of Mixed Data, a stimulus-sampled survey with a representative adult sample from French-speaking Switzerland, and cross-classified multilevel structural models linking design features to perceptions, attitudes, and behavioral intentions.

When Visualized Uncertainty Reduces and Increases Belief Update. This work examines the double effect of uncertainty displays: they can reduce belief update by lowering processing fluency, while also increasing belief update when they strengthen trust among more numerate audiences. The project uses preregistered online experiments with visualizations shown with or without uncertainty information, and tests the proposed mechanisms through parallel and moderated mediation models.

The Impact of Data Visualization on Beliefs and Opinions. This systematic literature review maps what can be claimed, and what remains uncertain, about the effects of data visualization on beliefs and opinions. It focuses on empirical human-participant studies using experimental or quasi-experimental designs, with transparent review procedures informed by PRISMA 2020, PRISMA-S, and PRISMA-P, and a planned structured narrative and framework synthesis of the evidence base.
